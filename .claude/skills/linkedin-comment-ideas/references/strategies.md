# The seven strategies

Apply exactly one strategy per comment variant. The acknowledgement (Part 1) is mandatory and lives in front of every strategy; the strategy (Part 2) is what follows it.

## Strategy 1 — Ask a clarifying question rooted in the topic

Use when the author makes a strong claim but leaves an interesting variable unaddressed. The question should be specific enough that answering it requires real thought — not a generic "tell me more."

*Example:* "You mentioned the new pipeline cut review cycles by 40%. Curious how that played out on the cost side — did inference costs land where you expected, or did the volume push you somewhere different?"

## Strategy 2 — Disagree and argue your position

Use when you have a real, evidence-backed counter-position. Never belittle the author. Never accuse. Acknowledge what's right first; then state your different experience and ask what made the difference.

*Example:* "Good point on the tool itself. We tried something similar last year and ran into [specific failure mode] — ended up rolling back. What do you think made the difference for your case?"

## Strategy 3 — Share personal experience (requires transcript evidence)

Use when Petro has a directly relevant story from his own work. **The story must come from a transcript or documented project history — never invented.** If no real example exists, pick another strategy.

Anchor candidates from Petro's history:

- BankAI (pre-ChatGPT production GenAI for German bank, sold 2023)
- True BDD prototype (in development; public release 16 May 2026)
- Speed & Function engagements (Wikimedia, Open Supply Hub)
- Earlier ventures (Crossroad Labs, others)

*Example:* "We tried a similar setup on BankAI back in 2022 — pre-ChatGPT, so we had to build the orchestration ourselves. First version missed [X] for the same reason you describe. What fixed it for us was [specific thing]; curious if you saw the same."

When this strategy is used, the output **must** include a citation note pointing to the transcript or document the story came from (filename + approximate section or timestamp).

## Strategy 4 — Ask a provocative question

Use when the post is reasonable but slightly under-examined. The question should pull the conversation into a sharper place — risks, blindspots, or contrarian framings — without being hostile.

*Example:* "The approach looks solid for current model capabilities. What's the failure mode you'd worry about if the underlying model shifts in the next 6 months? Anything in the design that wouldn't survive that?"

## Strategy 5 — Tag someone who can contribute

Use when the author is openly looking for a solution, opinion, or contact — and Petro knows someone (or knows of someone public) who genuinely fits. Only tag people Petro could credibly tag (someone in his network or a known public figure where the tag is contextually appropriate). Do not tag for visibility purposes alone.

## Strategy 6 — Reference prior-year data or trends

Use when the post contains analytics or claims a trend. Compare against a different period or dataset, propose a hypothesis for the shift, invite the author's read.

*Example:* "Interesting — the same metric was running in the opposite direction in 2024. My read is [hypothesis: e.g., the cost of inference came down enough to change the buy-vs-build calculus]. Curious how you'd explain the flip."

## Strategy 7 — Offer a supporting link or material

Use when you can point to a paper, post, or dataset that strengthens or complicates the author's point. Always **summarize the relevant finding in your own words first**, then link. Do not just drop a link.

Acceptable sources include: METR, CodeRabbit, Veracode, GitClear, arxiv, Latent Space, official company posts. Avoid linking to anything Petro hasn't actually read.
